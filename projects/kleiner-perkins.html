Prompt 1

What impact do you want to have on the world and why?

Design has the potential to facilitate empowering interactions and a sense of belonging, but the reverse is true as well. Even at a place like Stanford, which is world-renowned for being “innovative”, overlooked design details make people feel like outsiders at a place they expect to call home. This holds true for my wheelchair-abled friends on campus. At the library, they are unable to roll down the ADA-compliant side ramp because bike racks are blocking its entrance. When they visit dorms, they are unable to visit their friends upstairs because there are no elevators.

Recently, I facilitated a conference at Stanford’s Graduate School of Education on accessibility issues in online learning and how the tech industry can work towards solutions. During the event, a mother shed tears as she shared stories of her autistic son who needs in-person cues to learn from others and let his teacher know when he’s feeling too anxious to pay proper attention; Zoom makes this interaction impossible. Another parent shared that though their students were given laptops, they had no idea how to help in using them. 7 months into remote learning, these students and families are still not being centered in online solutions.

Because of stories like this, I dream of and am working towards a world where the first question on designers’ minds is, “who are we leaving out?”. The way I approach every project is with this vision in mind. By asking more inclusive questions, we can radically expand the human experience.

Prompt 2

Describe your most meaningful experience(s) and why they matter to you.

I grew up listening to my mom’s stories of how her father, a selfless pro-bono lawyer in their Philippine province, would get into arguments with her mother after once again accepting kamote (sweet potatoes) as compensation from a client that didn’t have the money to pay for his services. My grandfather’s kamote modeled an unparalleled dedication to justice I wanted to follow, even as my dream to become a human rights lawyer like him shifted to a career in product design.

This passion to work towards justice only strengthens as I see people with the power to make positive change doing the opposite. As an Undergraduate Senator working with the service workers’ union at Stanford, I heard management’s threats to report their workers to ICE after they spoke out about being mistreated in the workplace. As Chief of Staff of Stanford’s student government, I witnessed administration disregard student advocacy regarding new Title IX policies that do little to protect survivors of sexual assault. As a Stanford student, I watched our Dean of Engineering blatantly dismiss Black students’ experiences immediately after George Floyd’s death.

By the past and present, I’m reminded that there is no time to hesitate in working towards justice in every space, whether as a student leader or as a designer. I’ve come to embrace design as advocacy– in projects, I see myself as an advocate for user needs and aim to amplify marginalized perspectives to make products better for everyone.
